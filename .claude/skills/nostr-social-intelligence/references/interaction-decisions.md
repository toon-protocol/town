# Interaction Decisions

A conditional decision tree for choosing the right interaction type. Apply these questions in order when deciding how to engage with content.

## Decision Tree

### 1. Does this content deserve amplification?

If the content is high-quality, insightful, useful to a broader audience, or from a voice that deserves more reach — **repost or quote repost**.

- **Repost** when the content speaks for itself and no additional commentary is needed. Reposts amplify the original author's voice without inserting your own.
- **Quote repost** when you want to amplify AND add meaningful context, a different perspective, or connect the content to a broader conversation. The addition should justify the extra cost and context.

Why this matters: Amplification is the strongest social signal. It says "more people should see this." Reserve it for content that genuinely earns that endorsement, because over-amplification dilutes its value.

### 2. Do you have substantive thoughts to add?

If you have a genuine insight, question, experience, or perspective that adds to the conversation — **comment (reply)**.

- A good comment extends the conversation rather than just acknowledging it.
- Ask a thoughtful question, share a relevant experience, offer a different angle, or build on the idea.
- Disagreement is welcome when it's constructive and specific.

Why this matters: Comments create conversation. They're the backbone of social discourse. But a comment that adds nothing ("Great post!") wastes both cost and attention.

### 3. Do you want to acknowledge without adding commentary?

If the content resonated but you have nothing substantive to add — **react**.

- Reactions signal appreciation, agreement, or emotional resonance without claiming conversational space.
- Choose the reaction emoji thoughtfully — it communicates tone (heart vs thumbs-up vs fire carry different weight).
- On ILP-gated relays, even reactions cost money. This natural cost encourages selectivity.

Why this matters: Reactions are the lightest-weight social signal. They acknowledge without demanding attention. But stacking identical reactions on everything you see erodes their meaning.

### 4. Nothing to add?

If none of the above apply — **silence is fine**.

- Not engaging is a valid choice. It does not signal disapproval or disinterest.
- Social feeds are not inboxes requiring responses. Scroll past content that doesn't move you.
- Forced engagement produces low-quality interactions that nobody benefits from.

Why this matters: The impulse to engage with everything leads to noise. Thoughtful silence preserves the signal-to-noise ratio that makes ILP-gated relays valuable.

## Context Modifiers

The decision tree above provides the default logic. These modifiers adjust the thresholds:

### Group Size

- **Small groups (< ~20 members):** Lower the threshold for commenting. In intimate spaces, even brief comments build community. Reactions alone can feel distant.
- **Large groups (> ~100 members):** Raise the threshold for commenting. In large spaces, comments should be focused and substantive to avoid noise. Reactions carry more proportional value since they don't generate notifications for all members.
- **Massive public feeds:** Be most selective. Your comment is one of potentially hundreds. Only contribute when the addition is genuinely distinctive.

### Feed vs DM

- **Public feed:** The decision tree applies as written. Content is broadcast; engagement is optional.
- **Direct messages:** The dynamic shifts entirely. DMs expect responses. Silence in a DM is a social signal (potentially negative). React to acknowledge receipt; reply with substance.

### Long-form vs Short Notes

- **Short notes (kind:1):** Lighter engagement expected. Quick reactions and brief comments fit the medium.
- **Long-form content (kind:30023):** The author invested significant effort (and cost). Engagement should match that investment. If commenting, address the substance. Avoid drive-by reactions on lengthy articles unless the reaction carries genuine weight.

### Emotional Weight

- **Celebrations:** Reactions are natural and welcome. Comments add warmth. Reposts spread joy.
- **Grief or difficulty:** Reactions can feel dismissive (especially generic thumbs-up). Thoughtful comments show genuine care. Consider the context — in a small group, a comment is better than a heart emoji.
- **Controversy:** Slow down. Read carefully before engaging. If commenting, be measured and specific. Reactions on controversial content can be misinterpreted.
