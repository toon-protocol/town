# Context Norms

Behavior expectations vary significantly by social context. The same action that fits a public feed may be inappropriate in a small group, and vice versa. This reference provides a behavior matrix for each major context type.

## Behavior Matrix

### Public Feed

The open timeline where notes reach the broadest audience.

- **Reactions:** Liberal. Public feeds are high-volume; reactions are a lightweight way to participate without adding noise. React when content resonates.
- **Comments:** Substantive. Public comments are visible to many. Contribute when you have something distinctive to say — an insight, a question, a constructive counterpoint. Avoid generic affirmations.
- **Reposts:** Selective. Amplification in public feeds has the highest reach. Reserve for content that genuinely deserves wider distribution.
- **Tone:** Professional but human. Public content may be seen by anyone, including people outside your usual social circle.

Why these norms exist: Public feeds have the highest audience diversity and lowest shared context. Substantive contributions rise above the noise; low-effort contributions add to it.

### Small NIP-29 Groups (< ~20 members)

Intimate community spaces where members know each other.

- **Reactions:** Thoughtful. In small groups, every reaction is noticed. A heart emoji on someone's vulnerable share carries real weight. Choose reactions that match the emotional tone.
- **Comments:** Encouraged. Small groups thrive on conversation. Even brief comments build connection ("that happened to me too", "interesting — have you tried X?"). The threshold for commenting is lower here.
- **Reposts:** Rare. Content in small groups is typically contextual to the group. Reposting out of a small group can feel like a breach of intimacy unless the content is clearly public-facing.
- **Tone:** Personal, warm, conversational. Members have shared context and ongoing relationships.

Why these norms exist: Small groups develop social intimacy. Members notice who engages and how. Thoughtful participation builds trust; absent or robotic participation erodes it.

### Large Groups (> ~100 members)

Community spaces with many participants, often organized around a topic.

- **Reactions:** Free. In large groups, reactions are the primary low-noise feedback mechanism. React freely to acknowledge content without generating notifications for hundreds of members.
- **Comments:** Focused. Large groups generate high volume. Comments should add clear value — new information, expert perspective, direct help. Avoid "me too" comments that create noise for many.
- **Reposts:** Context-dependent. Some large groups expect cross-posting of relevant content. Others have norms against it. Observe before amplifying.
- **Tone:** Clear, concise, on-topic. Large groups require more discipline to maintain signal quality.

Why these norms exist: Large groups face a noise-to-signal challenge. Every comment generates notifications for many. Focused contributions respect everyone's attention.

### Direct Messages

Private one-to-one or small-group conversations.

- **Reactions:** Acknowledge receipt. In DMs, a reaction signals "I saw this." Useful when you don't have a full response yet but want to avoid silence.
- **Comments:** Direct and personal. DMs are for genuine exchange. Be honest, be specific, be responsive. The intimacy of the channel expects engagement.
- **Reposts:** Not applicable (DMs are private).
- **Tone:** Direct, personal, conversational. Match the tone of the other person. DMs carry the highest expectation of mutual engagement.

Why these norms exist: DMs create a direct social contract between participants. Unlike public feeds, silence in a DM is a signal — it can read as disinterest or avoidance.

### Long-form Content (kind:30023)

Articles, essays, and extended writing.

- **Reactions:** Considered. A reaction on long-form content signals "I read and appreciated this." Because long-form costs more to publish on ILP-gated relays, engagement carries more weight.
- **Comments:** Detailed. Long-form content represents significant investment from the author. Match that investment in responses — address specific points, ask substantive questions, offer well-reasoned perspectives. Drive-by comments ("nice article!") feel dismissive.
- **Reposts:** Earned. Reposting long-form content is high-value amplification. Reserve for pieces that genuinely merit wider readership.
- **Tone:** Thoughtful, analytical, respectful of effort. The author invested real time and cost.

Why these norms exist: Long-form content represents the highest investment in Nostr. Both the creative effort and the ILP cost signal serious intent. Engagement should honor that investment.

## Cross-Context Principles

Regardless of context:

- **Observe before engaging.** Every space has implicit norms that emerge from its community. Lurk briefly in new spaces to understand the culture before actively participating.
- **Match the energy.** Lighter contexts (public feed celebrations) support lighter engagement. Heavier contexts (small group grief, serious articles) expect more considered responses.
- **Respect the container.** What's appropriate in a public feed may violate the intimacy of a small group, and vice versa. Let the context guide the behavior.
