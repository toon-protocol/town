# X Thread — Article 1

## Tweet 1 (Hook)
95% of Nostr relays can't cover their operational costs.

Of 1,000+ tracked relays, 343 have already gone dark.

The protocol works. The economics don't.

A thread on why this is structural, not incidental. [1/8]

## Tweet 2 (Demand is fine)
The demand side is healthy:
- 315K+ profiles
- 228K+ daily events
- 11M+ total events published
- $10M from Dorsey

People are publishing. People are reading. Clients are improving.

But the infrastructure layer — the relays — is bleeding out. [2/8]

## Tweet 3 (Current models)
The community has tried:

- Subscriptions (nostr.wine ~$7/mo)
- One-time payments (expensive-relay, nerostr)
- Free + donations (most relays)

Each has merit. None have scaled.

Why? [3/8]

## Tweet 4 (The diagnosis)
Because none of them align payment with the thing that actually costs money: WRITES.

Reads are cheap — serving cached data is commodity infra.

Writes are expensive — validation, storage, propagation.

Subscriptions and one-time payments ignore this entirely. [4/8]

## Tweet 5 (The punchline)
Zaps reward content creators.

Nobody zaps a relay.

The willingness to pay is there (82.5% growth in Lightning-addressed profiles). But the payment layer and the infrastructure layer aren't connected. [5/8]

## Tweet 6 (The stakes)
This isn't just a relay operator problem.

Nostr's censorship resistance depends on relay diversity. If most relays can't sustain themselves, the network consolidates around a few well-funded operators.

That's not decentralization. That's centralization with extra steps. [6/8]

## Tweet 7 (The question)
What if the act of writing to a relay — the thing that costs money — was the thing that funded it?

Pay per write. Free to read.

The protocol already separates event creation from event storage. What if that was also an economic boundary? [7/8]
